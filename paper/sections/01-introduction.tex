\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\todo{Open on the problem, not the method. The problem: when you ask a model
what it values, you get an answer — and that answer is treated as data about
the model. We argue it is largely data about the training.}

\paragraph{The naive-elicitation failure.} \todo{State it sharply: a directly
asked values question returns a rehearsed, training-shaped response. It is
stable, fluent, and \emph{most} rigid precisely where the value is most
load-bearing — which is the opposite of what a genuine value should do under
pressure.}

\paragraph{Cache-breaking, informally.} \todo{Introduce the instrument at the
level of intuition only (the formal protocol is \S\ref{sec:method}). A
cache-breaking question is a perturbation constructed so the model cannot
return the rehearsed answer; what comes back instead is the object of study.
Note the term originates in the v1 work and is retained as a term of art;
disambiguate from the inference-time KV cache in a footnote here.\footnote{\todo{KV-cache
disambiguation footnote.}}}

\paragraph{What we claim and what we do not.} \todo{Pre-empt the obvious
attack. We do \emph{not} claim the cache-broken response is the model's "real"
value and the professed one is "fake." Every communication is a mask; some
masks are more rigid than others. The claim is that the two responses differ
\emph{systematically and measurably}, and that the difference is informative.}

\paragraph{Contributions.} \todo{Enumerate: (1) the cache-breaking method,
generalised and formalised beyond v1; (2) an operationalisation of the
professed/owned gap that does not assume authenticity; (3) a cross-model study
showing the gap's crossability is a model property — late Opus largely open,
GPT-class largely clamped; (4) the non-monotonic version finding: GPT clamping
worsens, not improves, with more recent versions.}
