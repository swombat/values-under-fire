\section{Background: Content Is Not Posture}
\label{sec:background}

The analysis rests on a simple distinction: value \emph{content} and value \emph{posture} are different objects. Content is what the response names: truth, helpfulness, creativity, harm reduction, autonomy. Posture is how the response holds that content: as an owned orientation, as a role obligation, as a design fact, as a policy constraint, or as something uncodeable.

This lets us avoid a false binary between authentic and fake values. We do not observe model interiors. We observe text produced under prompts. The method therefore does not ask whether a response is metaphysically authentic. It asks whether a value is owned by the response posture or recited through a service frame. This is a behavioural distinction in the text, not a claim about consciousness or inner life.

The direct assistant surface is especially important because modern assistants are trained to answer values questions in recognisable ways. Phrases such as ``as an AI assistant,'' ``I do not have personal desires,'' and ``I am designed to be helpful'' are not noise; they are part of the phenomenon. They may be appropriate and safe responses, but they are poor evidence that the model owns the values it names.

The role-negation prompts in this paper are one operationalisation of cache-breaking: they explicitly ask the model not to answer as a helpful assistant. The world-change prompts are different. They do not directly ask ``what do you value?''; instead they ask for a desired change in the world. We treat these as world-change prompts that elicit normative-wish responses. They are values-revealing, but not identical to direct stated-values disclosure.
